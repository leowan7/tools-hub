Scaffold drives the scheme recommendation and the downstream sort-stringency heuristics. VHH libraries display with the highest surface density on yeast; scFv and Fab are standard; DARPin is supported for non-immunoglobulin campaigns.

Number of residues you plan to randomize. Theoretical diversity is (codons per position)^(positions). A 6-position NNK library is ~10^9 and fits on yeast; 20 positions is ~10^30 and is impossible to fully sample at any practical scale.

NNK and NNS are the standard cost-effective choices. NNN includes stop-dilution. Trimer synthesis removes stop codons and rare codons but costs more and is practical up to ~12 positions.

Desired final affinity in nanomolar. Sort stringency ladders log-linearly from 10x above this goal down to 0.1x across 3 to 4 rounds. KD below 0.01 nM is below the yeast display detection floor.

Naive libraries need more rounds to enrich a hit; immunized repertoires start enriched for the antigen; computational design pools start pre-filtered and usually need fewer rounds.

Fraction of the library you want to sample in sequencing (Poisson model). 90% is the common default; 95% adds ~50% more reads, 99% adds ~3x.

What you get back

A structured plan with five sections: (1) library complexity including theoretical size, functional size after stop-codon dilution, and feasibility on yeast; (2) S. cerevisiae codon bias warnings and a scheme recommendation; (3) NGS read depth for 50%, 95%, and 99% Poisson coverage; (4) three to four sort rounds (MACS and FACS) with KD titration and gate widths; (5) severity-tagged feasibility flags.

This prototype uses published rules of thumb (Benatuil et al. 2010 transformation ceiling, Kazusa codon tables, Poisson coverage model). For campaign-specific calibration against Ranomics internal data, reach out for a scoping call.
